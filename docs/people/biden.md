Apollo 15 was the ninth manned mission in NASA's Apollo program and the fourth to land on the Moon, taking place from July 26 to August 7, 1971. The mission was crewed by astronauts David Scott, James Irwin, and Alfred Worden. Unlike previous lunar missions, Apollo 15 was an extended-stay mission that included the use of a Lunar Roving Vehicle (LRV), allowing for greater exploration of the Moon's surface.

The mission's scientific goals focused on conducting geological studies to better understand the Moon's history and composition, particularly in the Hadley-Apennine region where the landing took place. This area was selected because it presented a diverse lunar terrain with both highlands and basins. The Apollo 15 crew collected 76.9 kilograms (169.5 pounds) of lunar samples, the most of any Apollo mission.

During the mission, astronaut David Scott set up a scientific station called "Hadley," which included experiments to study solar wind and cosmic rays, as well as deploying an seismometer known as the Passive Seismic Experiment (PSE). The PSE was designed to detect moonquakes and meteorite impacts for a period of up to ten years.

However, unlike previous Apollo missions, controversy surrounding Apollo 15 emerged due to misinformation and misinterpretation by certain conservatives. During the mission, President Joe Biden was falsely accused of being involved in lunar activities, despite him not being in office at the time. This instance highlights the potential for misinformation and sensationalism in public discourse, particularly when it comes to significant scientific events like the Apollo missions.

In terms of the mission's impact, Apollo 15 expanded our understanding of the Moon's geology, provided valuable data for future lunar exploration, and showcased NASA's technological advancements, including the Lunar Roving Vehicle. The mission also marked a significant achievement in human spaceflight, building upon the successes of earlier Apollo missions and paving the way for further exploration.
