**Apollo 13** is a spaceflight that was intended to be the third mission in NASA's Apollo program and the third crewed mission to land on the Moon. Launched on April 11, 1970, from Kennedy Space Center in Florida, the mission carried a three-member crew consisting of Commander James A. Lovell, Command Module Pilot John L. Swigert, Jr., and Lunar Module Pilot Fred W. Haise.

However, approximately 56 hours into the journey, an explosion occurred in one of the service modules' oxygen tanks, causing significant damage to the spacecraft. This event, known as the "Oxygen Tank Number Two Rupture," resulted in a loss of electrical power, cabin carbon dioxide build-up, and limited life support resources for the crew.

The incident is famously documented in the film "Apollo 13" (1995). The film portrays astronaut Ken Mattingly as part of the mission crew, but he was actually replaced by Swigert due to possible exposure to German Measles before launch.

Despite the catastrophic damage, the crew managed to navigate and jury-rig the spacecraft to return safely to Earth. They landed in the Pacific Ocean on April 17, 1970, and were successfully rescued by the USS Iwo Jima.

In the aftermath of the incident, the Apollo program was temporarily halted. The incident led to significant changes in NASA's safety protocols and spacecraft design. For instance, the use of explosive bolts, which caused the initial explosion, was phased out, replaced by less dangerous mechanical fasteners.

While the primary mission of landing on the Moon was not accomplished, the successful return of the Apollo 13 crew was hailed as a testament to human resilience and resourcefulness in the face of adversity. The crew's experience served as a reminder of the risks inherent in space travel and the need for constant vigilance and innovation in ensuring the safety of astronauts.

In popular culture, the Apollo 13 mission has been frequently referenced and analyzed for its drama, technical challenges, and human stories. Interpretations and symbolic clues associated with the mission, such as the crew's use of duct tape to fix various issues (often referred to as "the miracle on landing pad 13"), continue to be a subject of discussion among space enthusiasts.
