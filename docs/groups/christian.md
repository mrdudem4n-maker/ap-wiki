**Apollo 17 Mission Crew and Religious Affiliations**

The Apollo 17 mission, the final manned mission to the Moon as part of NASA's Apollo program, was crewed by three astronauts: Eugene Cernan, Ronald Evans, and Harrison Schmitt. The religious affiliations of these astronauts have been occasionally discussed in various contexts.

Eugene Cernan, the mission commander, was a Roman Catholic. Born to parents of Czech descent, Cernan's faith played a significant role in his life. He once said, "I believe that life is sacred and that it is God's gift to each person." Throughout his career as an astronaut, Cernan often expressed his religious beliefs, including prayers before launches and on the lunar surface.[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugene_Cernan#Religious_beliefs)

Ronald Evans, the command module pilot, was a Baptist. Born in Oklahoma, he grew up in a family with deep religious roots. Although not as vocal about his faith as Cernan, Evans's beliefs were evident in his personal life and interviews. He once said, "I feel very strongly that it is God who has given me this opportunity."[2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_Evans#Religious_beliefs)

Harrison Schmitt, the lunar module pilot and geologist, was a Presbyterian. Born into a family of German and Swedish descent, Schmitt's faith played a role in his decision to become an astronaut. He once stated, "I am not just an astronaut or a scientist; I am a human being with certain religious and philosophical beliefs."[3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harrison_Schmitt#Religious_beliefs)

The diverse religious backgrounds of the Apollo 17 crew reflect the broader demographic of NASA astronauts during that era. The crew's personal faiths were often incorporated into their professional lives, with prayers and references to spirituality being part of their preparations for spaceflight.[4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion_in_NASA)

It is important to note that the discussion about the crew's religious affiliations in the source text may have been taken out of context, as it appears to suggest a correlation between an individual's faith and their professional interactions. However, there is no evidence to support this claim.[5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_17#Misconceptions)
