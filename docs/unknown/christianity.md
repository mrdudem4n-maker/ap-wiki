**Book of Apocephon of John**

The Book of Apocephon (or Apocephon) of John, also known as the Third Treatise or Second Apocalypse of John, is a late-first-century CE Gnostic text found among the Nag Hammadi library discovered in 1945. This text is not to be confused with the New Testament's Book of Revelation, which is attributed to John the Evangelist and Apostle.

The Book of Apocephon of John is a revelatory narrative written in the form of a dialog between the Archangel Michael (also referred to as Jeu) and the apostle John. It provides insights into the Gnostic view of the cosmic struggle between the divine and demonic forces, and presents a detailed account of the creation, fall, and redemption of the universe.

The text is believed to originate from Jewish-Christian circles in Egypt. The exact date of its composition is uncertain, but scholars typically place it around the late first or early second century CE. This places it roughly contemporaneous with other New Testament writings, but distinct in both its content and religious outlook.

The Book of Apocephon of John shares similarities with the Book of Enoch, an ancient Jewish text also concerned with cosmic struggles, divine revelation, and the fall of angels. However, it differs significantly in its theological perspectives, emphasizing a dualistic worldview that posits a struggle between the transcendent, spiritual realm (the Pleroma) and the material world (the Demiurge).

The text's significance lies in its contribution to the development of Gnostic thought. It offers unique insights into the cosmology, anthropology, and eschatology of one of the many Gnostic groups that flourished during the formative years of early Christianity. Understanding the Book of Apocephon of John helps shed light on the rich diversity of religious beliefs and practices coexisting within the religious landscape of the ancient Mediterranean world.
