Title: Satanic Panic

The Satanic Panic was a widespread contemporary moral panic during the late 20th century, primarily focused in North America and Western Europe, that involved claims of an international child-abusing network associated with Satanism or occult rituals.[1] The fear, fueled by various misconceptions and sensationalized media reports, led to numerous investigations, prosecutions, books, films, and legislative proposals related to alleged Satanic abuse and ritualistic violence.

The origins of the Satanic Panic can be traced back to the late 1970s and early 1980s. One of the key events that sparked this fear was the McMartin Pre-School trial in California, where accusations were made against employees for performing satanic rituals on children.[2] Despite the eventual dismissal of charges due to lack of evidence, it served as a catalyst for widespread concern about Satanism and child abuse.

The Satanic Panic had profound effects on popular culture and society. In the realm of entertainment, movies such as "The Exorcist" (1973) and books like Michelle Smith's "Michelle Remembers" (1980) contributed to the public's perception of a widespread satanic conspiracy.[3][4] These works were often misinterpreted as factual accounts, further perpetuating the panic.

In terms of legal consequences, numerous investigations and prosecutions ensued, many of which resulted in false accusations and wrongful convictions. The West Memphis Three case, for example, involved the conviction of three teenagers for the murder of eight-year-old boys in Arkansas in 1993.[5] Although they maintained their innocence and claimed the crime was motivated by Satanism, they were initially sentenced to life in prison. After evidence supporting their innocence emerged, they were eventually released in 2011 following a plea deal.

The Satanic Panic also led to the creation of various laws aimed at addressing perceived satanic activities, such as the Child Abuse Prevention and Treatment Act (CAPTA) of 1974.[6] However, these measures were often criticized for their overly broad definitions of child abuse, which could potentially ensnare innocent individuals.
